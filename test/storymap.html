Chapter 01 · The Origin

The Green Gold

Pu'er & Xishuangbanna

More than a millennium ago, this humid rainforest was the starting point of the longest trade route in the ancient world. Here, the muleteers (known as Mabang) compressed loose tea leaves into dense, round cakes to withstand the damp journey ahead.

Before departing, the caravan leader would offer incense to the gods. They faced a year-long trek, fearing not just the bandits, but the deadly "miasma" (malaria) of the tropical valleys. It was a gamble of life for prosperity.

Chapter 02 · The Hub

The Great Barter

Dali Ancient City

Arriving in Dali, the caravans entered a bustling world of commerce. This was the choke point where tea from the south met horses from the north. The Bai people facilitated a massive exchange—one warhorse was often worth 60kg of tea.

The markets were loud with bargaining in a dozen dialects. It was here that the muleteers restocked supplies, repaired their gear, and prepared for the ascent into the high altitude.

Chapter 03 · The Ascent

Into the Thin Air

Lijiang & Jade Dragon

Beyond Lijiang, the "Tea Horse Road" transformed from a dirt path into a treacherous lifeline carved into cliffs. The Naxi people called this the threshold of the heavens.

The Jade Dragon Snow Mountain loomed overhead. Many mules—and men—were lost here, slipping from icy, narrow ledges into the Jinsha River gorge below. It was a test of endurance against the elements.

Chapter 04 · The Sanctuary

A Spiritual Arrival

Shangri-La (Zhongdian)

Upon reaching the Tibetan plateau, the exhausting journey found its spiritual release. At the Songzanlin Monastery, tea was brewed into butter tea—a staple for survival in the cold.

Muleteers would offer part of their profits to the temples, grateful for surviving the journey. Here, commerce blended seamlessly with deep devotion.
